Quiet Bloom is a period-tracking app that helps users understand how mood and energy change across the menstrual cycle. Using previous months of data, it predicts upcoming periods and ovulation as well as expected mood, energy, and pain levels. Users can also write daily journal entries.

User Testing

Abstract

This user study was conducted to evaluate how well the features of the Quiet Bloom app are integrated and how useful they are for users. The study focused on the app’s usability, intuitiveness, and overall user experience. Participants tested the app and provided feedback on how easily they could understand and use its features, as well as how clearly predictions and visual elements were presented. The results helped identify strengths of the app and uncover areas where improvemnt was needed.

1. Introcuction

Quiet Bloom is a period tracker which allows users to not only log their period but also their painlevel, energylevel, mood and journal entry for everyday. But there is also the option to log the entire period with a multiselect tool for people who dont wanna input everyday. There is a quick overview on form of a calendar page where the user can see their last 6 months of periods and predictions of periods for the next 6 months as well as ovulation. Ther also is a additional page for analysis where the user can overview their bloodflow, mood, energy level and pain level of the current month but also see a prediction for all those catgories for the current and the next two months as well as a mood overview for the current month. Our app is for people who menstruate and want a deeper understanding of how their mood and energy levels fluctuate over the course of their cycle and also want precise predictions for ther bloodflow and pain levels.
Based on this we formulated following hypotheses:
H0: The app does not help users predict and understand their mood, pain, energy levels, and period flow in a simple and easy-to-understand way?
H1: Does the app help users predict and understand their mood, pain, energy levels, and period flow in a simple and easy-to-understand way?

3. User Study

This user study follows a relational, within-subject design and was conducted to evaluate the usability of the Quiet Bloom app. The independent variable of the study was the type of app used, while the dependent variable was the perceived usability of the app, including intuitiveness, ease of use, and clarity of predictions and visual elements. The study included nine participants, with a total of ten test sessions conducted over two rounds. All participants were between 20 and 25 years old, menstruating, and mostly familiar with period-tracking applications. Quantitative data was collected using the System Usability Scale (SUS) and the Single Ease Question (SEQ). In addition, qualitative data was gathered through think-aloud comments during task execution and semi-structured interviews, focusing on user feedback regarding predictions, statistics, visual design, and the perceived accuracy and usefulness of the app.

The usability tests were conducted as moderated sessions using a think-aloud protocol. Participants were asked to complete specific tasks while verbalizing their thoughts and impressions. After completing each task, participants filled out the SEQ, and after all tasks were completed, they filled out the SUS.

3.1 Test Round 1

The first test round focused on gathering initial feedback on the overall usability and design of the app. Participants were given a set of tasks to complete and were then given the opportunity to further explore the app during a short interview. The emphasis was on understanding how intuitive the app felt, how clearly features were presented, and how users percieved the visual design and general structure of the application.

Results and Comments

Results Test Round 1

During the first test round, we evaluated the main features and design choices.

Task 0 was designed to evaluate how accurately Quiet Bloom calculates the next period. Users were asked to input their last three periods into the calendar and then compare our prediction with their current period app. The means for each input scenario were: when only one month was entered, the prediction was on average 1 day off; with two months, it was 1.25 days off; and with three months, it was 1 day off.

Task 1 focused on adding an entry for the current day, including blood flow, pain, mood, energy, and a journal entry. The average SEQ score for this task was 6.8. Users mentioned that the current day was not clearly marked in the calendar, which made it harder to find.

Task 2 tested whether users could find statistics about their average cycle and period length. This task received an average score of 5.8, with the main concern being that the elements looked like input fields due to the styling.

Task 3 was designed to test whether users could find their journal entries overview. This task also received a score of 5.8. Users commented that the element looked like a header and therefore it was unclear that it was a clickable button.

Task 4 investigated whether the delete button on the add-entry page was too hidden. This turned out to be the case, with a difficulty rating of 5.8.

Task 5 evaluated how users felt about the graphs. They were rated with an average score of 5.8. Main comments were that the colors were too dull to differentiate, the graphs were a bit overwhelming to understand, and some axes were missing labels.

The last task tested whether users would find the settings. This task was rated with an average SEQ score of 6.2. The only complaints were that changes were saved automatically and that the options menu opened on the wrong side of the screen.

Interview feedback further suggested that displaying ovulation more clearly and adding a multi-select input tool for symptoms and states would be beneficial.

After the first completed test round, multiple features were changed. A multiselect tool was implemented, which makes it faster to track periods if users do not want to input every day separately. On the calendar page, we marked the current date and added a shortcut to quickly jump back to today. On the add-entry page, the delete button was changed to be more visible. The colors of the graphs were adjusted, and only the first two categories are shown when entering the page to make it less overwhelming. The graphs also received short descriptions. Additionally, multiple small design choices were refined to make the interface more intuitive.

Results Test Round 2

During the second test round, we evaluated the feedback that had been implemented since the first test.

Task 1 tested the multi-input tool. This task received an average SEQ score of 5.2. Users mentioned that the icon was not clear and that system visibility was not sufficiently communicated.

Task 2 was designed to assess whether the shortcut back to the current date was clear. This was rated very positively, with an average score of 7.

Task 3 checked whether it was clear what represents the actual period, what is a prediction, and what indicates ovulation. While most users found this understandable, one comment pointed out that users without prior experience with period apps might struggle. Therefore, adding a legend was suggested. This task received an average SEQ score of 6.8.

Task 4 evaluated whether the UI changes made it clearer where to find the journal entries. This task was rated with an average SEQ score of 6.8, showing an improvement compared to the first test round.

Task 5 examined whether the info boxes and filters being toggled off by default made the start screen clearer. This mostly succeeded, with an average SEQ score of 6.4.

After the second round, we made a few more adjustments. Both the calendar page and the add-entry page received info points to help less experienced users better understand their cycle. The color of the blood splatters on the calendar page was also improved to increase the visibility of dates.

SUS Score Comparison

Shapiro–Wilk tests indicated that SUS scores were not normally distributed for Test 1 (p = .041) and Test 2 (p = .046). Therefore, a Wilcoxon rank-sum test was conducted. The test showed no significant difference between SUS scores of Test 1 and Test 2 (W = 10.5, p = .749).

Although the quantitative results did not show a significant difference, participants who took part in both test rounds reported that the changes made to the app made it feel more comfortable to use.

Conclusion

Quiet Bloom was designed to help users better understand their menstrual cycle and how mood, energy, pain, and blood flow change over time through simple tracking and clear visualizations. The user study showed that participants generally found the app intuitive, helpful, and easy to use, supporting our hypothesis that the app can assist users in predicting and understanding their cycle in a simple and understandable way.

While statistical analysis of SUS scores did not show a significant difference between the two test rounds, qualitative feedback and task-based results indicate that the implemented changes improved the overall user experience. Features such as the multiselect tool, clearer delete button, shortcut to the current date, adjusted graph colors, and reduced visual complexity helped make the interface feel more comfortable and less overwhelming.

Some limitations of this study include the small sample size and the fact that most participants were already familiar with period-tracking apps, but overall Quiet Bloom demonstrates strong potential as a supportive and user-friendly period-tracking app that focuses not only on cycle prediction but also on emotional and physical well-being.
